Theory of change
Working backwards from civic impact
Using Pravah's "5th Space" framing — outcomes defined first, activities designed to serve them.

Preconditions & assumptions

A neutral physical space exists; at least 2 trained volunteer facilitators; a local institution (school, panchayat, or NGO) provides an anchor partnership.

Design principles from existing models

Pravah

Safe, non-judgmental space ("5th Space") with reflective practice as the pedagogic core — not information delivery.

ComMutiny

Youth-as-change-agents framing. Asset-based, not deficit — the young person is a resource to communities, not a problem to be solved.

India Fellow

Learning through doing. Structured reflection journals and peer learning circles as the engine of growth.

Barefoot College

Non-formal education works at scale. The rural/semi-urban context is an asset, not a constraint on quality.

YouthBuild (USA)

Service + skill + leadership, combined. Alumni return as next-cohort facilitators — the self-sustaining loop.

Hub model
How the hub runs without a paid coordinator
Designed from the ground up for zero full-time staff — powered by rotating peer leadership and alumni re-investment.

🏫 Physical space

Partner with a government school, library, or community hall for 2 evenings per week and Saturday mornings. MOUs, not cash leases. Minimum: one room, chairs, whiteboard, Wi-Fi.

👤 Membership

Open to 15–24 year-olds. No fees for the first cohort. Subsequent cohorts pay ₹100/month (waivable) — creating psychological ownership, not revenue. Cohort size: 20–35 members.

🔄 Self-sustaining loop

Cohort 1 alumni become Cohort 2 peer-facilitators. They receive a ₹500/month facilitator stipend crowd-funded from alumni dues and local business. No external coordinator after Month 10.

🛡 Political neutrality

Hub charter bans partisan activity. Civic dialogue follows structured protocols (not debate format). Facilitators trained in facilitation-not-persuasion. All external speakers sign a neutrality pledge.

The 90-day member journey

01

Days 1–30 · Orientation & identity

Welcome circle → community mapping → "who am I as a civic actor?" reflection → peer-pair assignment → first civic dialogue

02

Days 31–60 · Skills & exposure

Choose 2 skill sprints (public speaking / digital literacy / financial basics / gender equity) → field immersion with a local institution

03

Days 61–90 · Action & reflection

Small team designs and executes one community action project → public presentation → "My Civic Commitment" pledge ceremony

Gender inclusion — non-negotiables

  • Minimum 50% women in every cohort — actively recruited, not just welcome.
  • Women-only "safe circle" sessions held monthly alongside mixed sessions.
  • At least one woman in every governance body at all times (charter-mandated).
  • Sessions scheduled to avoid conflict with domestic responsibilities — weekend mornings preferred.
  • Female facilitators recruited and trained first; they anchor the women's cohort.
Governance
Flat, rotational, volunteer-led
No single point of failure. Every role has a successor pipeline baked in.
RoleCountTermResponsibilitiesConstraint
Hub Steward 2 6 months, rotating Chairs weekly sessions, signs MOUs, manages bank account (dual signatory) Cannot serve consecutive terms
Programme Lead 2 6 months Owns activity calendar and session quality. Recruits and briefs facilitators. Post-session check-ins. Can overlap with Steward only in first 3 months
Finance Keeper 1 12 months Tracks all income/expenses in a shared Google Sheet visible to all members. Monthly one-page financial summary. Cannot approve own expenses
Community Liaison 1 12 months Manages relationship with host institution and local government. Keeps a stakeholder register updated quarterly. Must disclose any conflicts of interest in writing
Alumni Council Advisory Open Past members who completed 2+ cohorts. No voting power but right to attend all meetings. Provides institutional memory. Cannot take operational roles while on council

Decision-making protocol

  • Routine (session topics, guest speakers): Programme Lead decides, informs at next meeting.
  • Resources above ₹5,000: Both Stewards + Finance Keeper must agree.
  • Charter amendments: ¾ majority of active members at a called meeting.
  • Conflict resolution: 3-person panel — 1 Steward + 1 Alumni + 1 member elected on the day.

Accountability mechanisms

  • Monthly open finances on shared Google Sheet — any member can view.
  • Quarterly "hub health check" — 10-question survey completed anonymously by all members.
  • Annual stakeholder meet with community audience — open to non-members.
  • Public impact report published each December — one page, member-authored.

Fundraising principles

  • No single funder above 40% — preserves programmatic independence.
  • All sponsorships in a public register; no anonymous donors.
  • Business sponsors get recognition, not programming influence. Separate acknowledgement from endorsement.
  • Panchayat grants tied to activity reports, not ideology or political alignment.
  • After Year 1, target one FCRA-registered NGO co-host to unlock grant access.
  • Alumni dues create psychological ownership and community accountability — not primarily revenue.

Replication checklist
Any volunteer team can launch using this alone
20 steps across 4 phases. Check each off as you go — progress saves in this session.
0 of 20 steps completed
Phase A · Before launch (Weeks 1–4)
Identify and meet 5 potential youth members from the target town
Secure a physical space via MOU — school, library, or community hall
Recruit 2 volunteer facilitators and run a 2-day facilitator training
Identify a local institution anchor (NGO, panchayat, school) as partner
Open a zero-balance bank account in the hub's name (dual signatory)
Phase B · Launch (Weeks 5–8)
Co-create the hub charter with founding members (neutrality clause mandatory)
Enrol 20–35 members; collect signed membership forms
Elect first Hub Stewards and Programme Leads via open vote
Set up shared Google Sheet for finances — visible to all members
Secure first 3 months of funding before announcing cohort start date
Phase C · First 90 days
Complete 12 weekly sessions following the 90-day journey structure
Run all 4 skill sprints with at least 70% member attendance
Execute at least 1 community action project with external stakeholders
Hold mid-cohort reflection retreat — 4–6 hours, off-site if possible
Run Cohort 1 graduation with community audience of 30+ people
Phase D · Sustainability (Months 4–12)
Convert at least 5 Cohort 1 alumni into Cohort 2 peer facilitators
Diversify funding to 3+ sources; no single source above 40%
Publish a one-page annual impact report (member-led, publicly accessible)
Complete self-assessment against success metrics; share with endorsing org
Share replication kit and lessons with 2 partner towns or organisations
